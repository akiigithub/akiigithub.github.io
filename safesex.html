WHAT IS SAFE SEX?

May 3rd 2023 5 Min read

Safe sex is any sexual contact that protects you and your sexual partner/s against sexually transmissible infections (STIs) and unplanned pregnancy. It doesn’t involve the exchange of body fluids like semen, vaginal fluids or blood with anyone you have sex with.

Unprotected Sex may put you at risk of STIs

Unsafe sex may put you or your sexual partners at risk of STIs. This does not just mean genital sex but any form of sexual contact (this includes anal, oral, vaginal and some skin-to-skin contact).

Certain STIs (such as syphilis and genital warts) can spread by having sex with an infected partner when a sore or rash is present.

Unborn babies are also at risk too, because some STIs like congenital syphilisExternal Link and HIV, can pass from mother to baby during pregnancy and at birth. If left untreated, STIs can cause serious illness and have long term impacts on your health (including pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) and infertility in men and women. STIs are common.

Some Types of STI's Include:


If sexually active, take these steps to protect from getting or spreading HPV, genital warts and other STIs:


• chlamydia

• gonorrhoea

• syphilis

• genital warts

• genital herpes

• HIV

• hepatitis A, B and C

• mycoplasma genitalium


Not all STIs have obvious symptoms so you and your sexual partners may not be aware that you have an STI. That’s why practising safe sex is important. Use condoms for safer sex.


Condoms (also called sheaths or rubbers) provide the best protection against STIs. They act as a physical barrier to prevent the exchange of body fluids. Although there is no guarantee that condoms and other barrier methods provide 100% protection against STIs, they help sex to be safer when used correctly.


Safe Sex for Sex Workers


Sex workers are at a high risk of sexually transmitted infections (STIs), including HIV, due to unprotected sexual activity and co-factors. However, many STIs can be easily cured with antibiotics. In order to reduce the transmission of STIs and HIV, it is important to eliminate STIs, particularly in commercial sex contacts where condoms are not used. This paper discusses two strategies that have been effective in treating STIs among female sex workers and their clients.


The first strategy involves clinical services that provide regular screening and a strong emphasis on consistent condom use. These services also empower sex workers through peer education and address larger social, economic, and human rights issues that increase their vulnerability to STIs and HIV. This strategy has been successful in increasing condom use and reducing the prevalence of STIs and HIV.


The second strategy is presumptive treatment of sex workers, which involves treating them for STIs even if they do not exhibit symptoms. This is a short-term measure that can rapidly reduce STI rates, but longer-term strategies are necessary once prevalence rates have been brought down. Effective preventive and curative STI services for sex workers are crucial for controlling the spread of STIs, including HIV, and are highly complementary to other HIV prevention efforts.
